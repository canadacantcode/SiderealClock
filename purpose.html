Our Purpose

The Karl Jansky Memorial, located next to Nokia Bell Labs Holmdel (40.36518° N, 74.16357° W), commemorates the discovery of the field of radio astronomy. At this location in 1931, Karl Jansky, a Bell Laboratories physicist and radio researcher, recorded for the first time radio signals from beyond the Earth. The source of these signals—radio noise of a wavelength of 14.6 meters—was the center of our Milky Way galaxy.

Marvin Skellet, a Bell Labs engineer, later observed that the signal repeated every sidereal day rather than every solar day — revealing that its origin was not terrestrial but galactic.

This sculpture commemorates Jansky’s discovery, first announced in 1933, which gave birth to the science of radio astronomy. The sculpture is oriented as Jansky’s antenna was at 7:10 p.m. on September 16, 1932, when the center of our galaxy came into view in the direction of the constellation Sagittarius A.

Karl Jansky died in 1950, years before the full importance of his discovery was recognized. In 1954, the International Astronomical Union named the “jansky” in his honor. His work paved the way for major astronomical discoveries such as quasars, radio galaxies, and cosmic jets. In 1964, Bell Labs scientists Penzias and Wilson used the nearby Holmdel Horn Antenna to detect the cosmic microwave background, a discovery that reshaped modern cosmology.
