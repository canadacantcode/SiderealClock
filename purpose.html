Our Purpose

The Karl Jansky Memorial, located next to Nokia Bell Labs Homedel (40.36518° N, 74.16357° W), commemorates the discovery of the field of radio astronomy. At this location in 1931, Karl Jansky, a Bell Laboratories physicist and radio researcher, recorded for the first time radio signals from beyond the Earth. The source of these signals—radio noise of a wavelength of 14.6 meters—was the center of our Milky Way galaxy. Marvin Skellet, a Bell Labs engineer, later observed that the signal repeated every sidereal day rather than every solar day — revealing that its origin was not terrestrial but galactic.

This sculpture commemorates Jansky’s discovery, first announced in 1933, which gave birth to the science of radio astronomy. The sculpture is oriented as Jansky’s antenna was at 7:10 p.m. on September 16, 1932, a date and time when the antenna’s directional antenna rotated, and the center of our galaxy came into view in the direction of the constellation Sagittarius A on the southern horizon.

Radio astronomy pioneer Karl Jansky died in 1950, years before the scientific significance of his discovery was fully recognized. In 1954, the International Astronomical Union gave his name to the international unit used to describe Jansky’s work led to major discoveries in astronomy, like the discovery of quasars, radio galaxies, and cosmic radio jets. In 1964, the Nobel Prize-winning discovery by Bell Laboratories scientists of the cosmic microwave background, which provided crucial understanding of the origin of our universe.
